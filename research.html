Research

I build machine learning that runs on physical hardware: diffusion-based samplers on an optical Ising machine at Sandia National Laboratories, and CUDA pipelines that extract signals at the quantum noise limit. Behind this sits a decade of physics, from quantum optics theory to spin-qubit experiments, across ten journal publications. My aim now is to carry that grounding into ML itself, toward capable and efficient models and AI agents. Below are the major projects in reverse chronological order, followed by the full publication list.

Projects

Amortized sampling for Boltzmann machines on an optical Ising machine

Sandia National Laboratories, Center for Integrated Nanotechnologies · 2026 – ongoing

Sampling from energy-based models like Boltzmann machines is a core bottleneck in generative modeling. I am building an amortized sampler based on correlated diffusion, realized on a continuous-variable optical Ising system engineered for fully frustrated (spin-glass) couplings — using physical hardware to attack hard sampling and combinatorial optimization problems that are expensive for conventional Monte Carlo methods.

Diffusion models Energy-based models Ising optimization Optical computing PyTorch

Near real-time faint-object detection on GPUs

Zetascope LLC · 2026

Detecting faint moving objects in image streams where the per-frame signal sits below the noise floor requires integrating intensity along millions of candidate trajectories — a brute-force compute problem. I developed a low-power, field-deployable detection pipeline that runs in near real-time on quantum shot-noise-limited images, hand-optimizing CUDA C++ kernels on the NVIDIA Blackwell architecture for a ~108× runtime reduction, with memory-hierarchy and roofline analysis guiding the optimization.

CUDA C++ Kernel optimization Roofline analysis Edge deployment

Deep-microscopic optical band theory of chiral tellurium

Purdue University, Jacob Group · 2024 – 2025 · Phys. Rev. B Letter, Editor's Suggestion

Tellurium's optical activity is known experimentally to diverge near the band edge, but decades of first-principles attempts failed to reproduce it because optical gyrotropy is intrinsically a finite-momentum effect. I built the first fully quantum, lattice-resolved optical band structure of chiral tellurium, solved the dispersion problem with quantitative agreement against experiment across the mid-infrared, and uncovered hidden optical waves and optical spin textures inside the lattice.

Alongside the theory, I co-authored Purdue-PicoMax, an open-source C++ package for nonlocal optical response in crystals that reaches ~5× lower error than existing methods; its predictions were later validated by electron-energy-loss measurements.

First-principles theory C++ scientific software Sparse eigensolvers Condensed-matter optics

Quantum theory of structured light

Purdue University, Jacob Group · 2022 – 2024 · New J. Phys. & JOSA B

Settled a long-standing debate on the correct quantum commutation relations for the angular momentum of light — a result with a widely propagated textbook error at its center — in a tutorial that was JOSA B's most-downloaded article for three consecutive months. Built the first quantum field theory of spatiotemporal optical vortices beyond the paraxial limit, predicting a previously unrecognized, experimentally accessible quantum fluctuation in their orbital angular momentum.

Quantum field theory Quantum optics Orbital angular momentum

Quantum sensing with NV centers in diamond

Purdue University, Jacob Group · 2021 – 2022 · multiple co-authored articles

Built experimental infrastructure for single spin-qubit control and readout: co-designed a cryogenic confocal microscope for optically-detected magnetic resonance, created shallow NV defects (~30 nm from the surface) via ion implantation and annealing, and developed a GHz phase/amplitude modulation setup for spin-textured light using temperature-controlled fiber Sagnac interferometry. This infrastructure underpins published measurements of photonic spin texture with NV-center quantum sensors.

Spin qubits ODMR Cryogenics Nanofabrication Interferometry

Microwave resonators for cryogenic spin spectroscopy

IISER Kolkata, Mitra Group · 2019 – 2021 · J. Phys. D & IOP SciNotes

Designed (CST Studio), fabricated (UV photolithography), and characterized on-chip microwave resonators — a short-circuited coplanar waveguide and a localized spoof-surface-plasmon structure — as the field-concentrating elements of home-built electron spin resonance spectrometers operating from room temperature down to 10 K.
